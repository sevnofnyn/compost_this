About Can I Compost This?

Who

This site is intended for a novice to intermediate composter. Future versions will add in more advanced knowledge.

What

Maybe you are wondering what the symbols mean when you enter in a search term. Here is a key to help.

  • This means you can put it in your worm bin,but please do not overfeed your worms.

  • This is appropriate for your backyard bin.

  • This item can go in your curbside bin for collection, if no other option.

  • This item should not go in the compost. Please dispose of according to your local authority's rules.

Why

I noticed a lot of information for composting is in static form and not a searchable database. The way we get our information has changed a lot. We might be out in the yard or in the kitchen and wondering if an item can be composted or not, and if you are like me, you have several different options for composting. Instead of looking for a book, doing an internet search, or picking up the phone, I wanted a quick resource to look up an item. I will be starting with more basic items and adding to the database. You can also contact me via the form on the website and ask about a particular item

Composting is also important because it saves a valuable resource from ending up in the landfill or incinerator. Your food scraps and other items can break down and become dirt!

Where

You can compost inside your home, on your porch, in your backyard, and curbside with municipal collection. Of course there are a lot of variables, such as does your city or hauler provide collection of compostables, does your community have rules or prohibitions regarding backyard composting (and if they do, I highly suggest you contact the folks who are in charge of those rules, be it a homeowner's association or your city council and ask/persuade/write compelling arguments about why they should allow composting or provide pickup. And as far as worms go, I confess it took several years before I was willing to share my home with a bunch of worms in a bin. But I am sure glad I did. Their poop is great for my potted plants and garden.

When

Composting can be done almost anywhere. If you live somewhere with sometimes arctic winters like I do, the process is a little different for the worms and for my backyard bin. First, I have to bring in my worms from the porch. They don't like temperatures below 40F/5C. Also they don't really like it above 80F/26C, so please keep them in the shade if they are outside in the heat.

Also, your backyard bin will slow down the breakdown process in the winter if it gets cold like it does in Minnesota. You can still choose to add to it, but understand your bin will fill up quite a bit before it can start breaking down again in the spring/summer.
